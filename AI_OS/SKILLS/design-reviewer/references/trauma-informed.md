# Trauma-Informed Design

For designs involving sensitive topics: abuse, health crises, financial distress, grief, violence, intimate content, addiction, mental health, legal troubles, etc.

## Core Principles (SAMHSA Framework)

### 1. Safety
**Users must feel physically and emotionally secure.**

Design Implications:
- Clear, calm visual language (avoid alarming colors/imagery)
- No unexpected sounds, autoplay, or jarring animations
- Content warnings before potentially triggering material
- Easy exit points throughout the experience
- Secure, private experience (no social sharing prompts)
- Clear data privacy messaging

Questions:
- Could any element startle or alarm a stressed user?
- Can users easily leave if overwhelmed?
- Is the visual tone calming or anxiety-inducing?

### 2. Trustworthiness & Transparency
**Build trust through clarity and honesty.**

Design Implications:
- Plain language (no jargon, legalese)
- Clear explanation of what happens at each step
- Honest about limitations and what you can/can't do
- No manipulative patterns (dark patterns)
- Transparent about data handling
- Consistent, predictable interface behavior

Questions:
- Do users know exactly what to expect?
- Is anything hidden or unclear?
- Would a distressed user feel manipulated?

### 3. Choice & Control
**Users maintain autonomy over their experience.**

Design Implications:
- Optional fields, not forced disclosure
- Clear "skip" or "not now" options
- Progress can be saved and returned to
- Users control the pace (no timers, urgency)
- Choice over communication preferences
- Easy way to undo or go back

Questions:
- Can users proceed at their own pace?
- Are there pressure tactics that need removal?
- Do users feel in control?

### 4. Collaboration & Mutuality
**Design with users, not for them.**

Design Implications:
- Language positions user as partner, not patient/victim
- Acknowledge user's expertise in their own situation
- Provide options rather than prescriptions
- Support different paths to the same goal
- Enable sharing with trusted supporters (proxy modes)

Questions:
- Does the language empower or patronize?
- Are multiple valid approaches supported?

### 5. Empowerment
**Prioritize user strengths and agency.**

Design Implications:
- Celebrate progress, even small steps
- Strength-based language ("You have rights" not "You're a victim")
- Clear, achievable next steps
- Resources for self-education
- Avoid learned helplessness patterns

Questions:
- Does the user feel capable after using this?
- Is the language empowering?

### 6. Cultural Sensitivity
**Respect diverse backgrounds and experiences.**

Design Implications:
- Inclusive language and imagery
- Consider varying cultural contexts
- Accessibility for all abilities
- Multiple language options where possible
- Avoid assumptions about family, relationships, circumstances

Questions:
- Could any content alienate or exclude?
- Are assumptions baked into the design?

## Visual Design for Sensitive Topics

### Color Considerations
- **Avoid**: Harsh reds, alarming yellow/black, clinical white
- **Prefer**: Muted, warm tones; soft greens/blues for calm; warm neutrals
- **Exception**: Red appropriate for genuine emergencies/crisis resources

### Typography
- **Avoid**: ALL CAPS (reads as shouting), tiny text, decorative fonts
- **Prefer**: Clean, readable fonts; generous line height; comfortable sizing

### Imagery
- **Avoid**: Graphic/explicit imagery; stock photos of distress; clichéd victim imagery
- **Prefer**: Abstract/illustrative; nature; calm spaces; diverse representation
- **Consider**: Do you need images at all? Sometimes text is less triggering

### Tone of UI
- Professional but warm
- Serious but not clinical
- Supportive but not pitying
- Informative but not overwhelming

## Content Patterns

### Information Disclosure
- Lead with what users need to know first
- Progressive disclosure for detailed information
- Clear section headings for scanning
- Bulleted lists over dense paragraphs

### Sensitive Content Handling
- Content warnings before potentially triggering material
- Allow users to skip or come back later
- Provide exit/support resources nearby
- Don't require viewing sensitive content

### Crisis Resources
- Prominently placed but not alarming
- Always visible, never hidden
- Include multiple resource types (phone, text, chat)
- Keep updated and functional

### Forms for Sensitive Information
- Explain why information is needed
- Make clear what's required vs. optional
- Never ask for the traumatic content itself
- Allow saving progress
- Confirm before submission

## Copy Guidelines

### Empowering Language
- "You have the right to..." not "Victims can..."
- "Take action" not "Submit"
- "Your options" not "You must"
- "Many people experience..." not "Victims often..."

### Trauma-Sensitive Phrasing
- "What happened to you" not "What you did"
- "Shared without consent" not "Leaked"
- "Your experience" not "Your incident"
- Active voice, user as subject

### Avoiding Re-traumatization
- Don't require retelling/reliving the experience
- Acknowledge difficulty without dwelling on it
- Focus on present action and future possibility
- Provide clear, practical steps

## Red Flags to Avoid

- [ ] Urgency/scarcity tactics ("Act now or else...")
- [ ] Forced sharing or social proof ("Tell us your story")
- [ ] Graphic imagery or detailed descriptions
- [ ] Clinical/medical language
- [ ] Victim-blaming language patterns
- [ ] Requiring unnecessary personal details
- [ ] No clear exit or pause options
- [ ] Missing crisis resources
- [ ] Autoplay audio/video
- [ ] Surprise content changes
- [ ] Dark patterns of any kind
- [ ] Assumptions about user's situation

## Evaluation Questions

For each design element, ask:
1. Could this trigger or re-traumatize someone?
2. Does this respect user autonomy?
3. Is this transparent and honest?
4. Does this empower or disempower?
5. Could a user in crisis safely navigate this?
6. Would someone feel worse after encountering this?
