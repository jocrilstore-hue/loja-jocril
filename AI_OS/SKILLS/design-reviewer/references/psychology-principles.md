# 100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know About People

Susan Weinschenk's psychology cheat codes for design. Research-backed principles organized by how humans see, read, remember, think, focus, decide, and feel.

---

## How People See

### Peripheral Vision Dominates

**People use peripheral vision to get the gist.** Central vision handles details; peripheral vision scans the environment.

- Peripheral vision processes faster (80ms vs 140-190ms for central)
- Peripheral vision detects danger, movement, emotion
- This is why flashing/moving ads in sidebars are so distracting

**Design implications:**
- Put critical info in central focus area
- Use peripheral areas for context, not critical actions
- Movement in periphery will steal attention—use sparingly

---

### Pattern Recognition

**The brain constantly seeks patterns.** We see patterns even when they don't exist (faces in clouds, etc.).

- Objects grouped together are perceived as related
- Visual proximity = conceptual relationship
- The Gestalt principles are hardwired

**Design implications:**
- Group related items visually
- Use consistent spacing to show relationships
- Leverage patterns people already know (conventions)

---

### The Face Effect

**Faces grab attention automatically.** There's a dedicated brain region (fusiform face area) just for processing faces.

- Faces evoke emotional responses
- People follow the gaze direction of faces in images
- Even abstract face-like patterns trigger this (: ))

**Design implications:**
- Use faces to draw attention to key content
- Have faces look toward your CTA or important info
- Avoid faces looking away from the action you want

---

### Change Blindness

**People miss changes in their visual field.** The famous "gorilla experiment"—50% of people watching a video missed a gorilla walking through because they were focused on counting passes.

**Design implications:**
- Don't assume users will notice changes you make
- Highlight changes explicitly (animations, color shifts)
- Don't rely on subtle visual updates for important info

---

### Red + Blue = Eyestrain

**Red and blue together are hard on the eyes.** Chromostereopsis—the colors appear at different depths, causing strain.

**Design implications:**
- Never put red text on blue background (or vice versa)
- Avoid red/blue adjacent elements
- 9% of men and 0.5% of women are colorblind—don't rely on color alone

## How People Read

### Screen Reading Is Harder

**Reading screens is ~25% slower than paper.** More fatigue, more errors, less comprehension.

**Design implications:**
- Increase font size for screen (16px minimum for body)
- Increase line spacing
- Keep text blocks shorter than print

---

### Line Length Paradox

**People read faster with longer lines but prefer shorter ones.**

- Optimal reading speed: ~100 characters per line
- Optimal preference: 45-75 characters per line
- People feel more comfortable with shorter lines even though they read slower

**Design implications:**
- For usability/comfort: 45-75 characters (use max-width: 65ch)
- For speed/efficiency: can go longer, but users may complain
- Decide based on whether preference or performance matters more

---

### Uppercase Is Not Harder

**UPPERCASE IS NOT INHERENTLY HARDER TO READ.** This is a myth. What matters is familiarity.

- We read by recognizing word shapes
- Unfamiliar formats slow us down
- Mixed case is faster because we're more practiced at it

**Design implications:**
- Use uppercase sparingly (headlines, labels)
- Never use uppercase for body text (not because it's harder, but because it signals SHOUTING)

---

### Reading ≠ Comprehending

**People can read without understanding.** Reading is automatic; comprehension requires effort.

**Design implications:**
- Don't assume people understand just because they read
- Use multiple formats: text + visuals + examples
- Test comprehension, not just readability

---

## How People Remember

### The Magic Number: 4

**Short-term memory holds about 4 items.** Not 7 (that was debunked). Four chunks.

**Design implications:**
- Limit navigation items to ~4 main categories
- Chunk phone numbers: 415-555-1234 not 4155551234
- Group form fields into ~4 sections max

---

### Recognition > Recall

**It's easier to recognize than to remember.** Recognition: "Is this your password?" Recall: "What's your password?"

**Design implications:**
- Show options rather than requiring users to type from memory
- Use dropdowns, suggestions, recent items
- Provide visual cues to trigger recognition

---

### Memory Is Reconstructive

**People don't replay memories—they rebuild them.** Each recall changes the memory slightly. This is why eyewitness testimony is unreliable.

**Design implications:**
- Don't trust user recall in research ("What did you do?")
- Observe behavior directly
- Recognize that what users say they did ≠ what they actually did

---

### Information Must Be Used to Stick

**Working memory → long-term memory requires rehearsal.** Information that's not used is forgotten.

**Design implications:**
- Get users to interact with information
- Provide immediate opportunities to apply what they learned
- Repetition and practice beat passive reading

## How People Think

### Progressive Disclosure

**People do the least work possible.** Show just enough info for the current decision; reveal more on demand.

**Design implications:**
- Don't frontload all information
- Hide advanced options behind "More" or "Advanced"
- Each step should have just enough info to continue

---

### Mental Models

**People have internal models of how things work.** When the design matches their mental model, it feels intuitive.

**Design implications:**
- Research users' existing mental models
- Match your design to their expectations
- If you must break mental models, make it very obvious

---

### Chunking

**Information is processed in bite-sized chunks.** Breaking complex info into smaller pieces reduces cognitive load.

**Design implications:**
- Break long forms into steps
- Use clear section headers
- Limit paragraphs to 3-4 sentences

---

### Stories Beat Data

**Narratives persuade more than statistics.** Stories activate more brain regions and trigger emotional responses.

**Design implications:**
- Lead with stories, support with data
- Use customer testimonials (stories) not just metrics
- Frame information as narrative when possible

---

### Confirmation Bias

**People seek info that confirms existing beliefs.** They ignore or discount contradictory evidence.

**Design implications:**
- Understand users' existing beliefs
- Frame new info in terms of what they already believe
- Don't expect facts alone to change minds

---

## How People Focus Attention

### Attention Is Selective

**People can only focus on one thing at a time.** Multitasking is a myth—it's actually rapid task-switching with cognitive costs.

**Design implications:**
- Don't expect users to track multiple things
- Remove distractions from key tasks
- One primary action per screen/section

---

### Sustained Attention: ~10 Minutes

**People can maintain focused attention for about 7-10 minutes.** Then it drops significantly.

**Design implications:**
- Break long content into <10 minute segments
- Provide breaks or topic shifts
- Most important info should come early

---

### What Grabs Attention

**Hardwired attention-grabbers:**
1. **Danger** - Threats, warnings
2. **Food** - Images of food are compelling
3. **Sex** - Sexual imagery is attention-grabbing
4. **Movement** - Animation, video
5. **Faces** - Human faces, especially eyes
6. **Stories** - Narrative structure

**Design implications:**
- Use these sparingly—they're powerful but can be manipulative
- Faces and stories are the most ethical to leverage
- Movement should be intentional, not decorative

---

### The Novelty Effect

**People habituate to repeated stimuli.** The first notification is exciting; the hundredth is noise.

**Design implications:**
- Vary your approaches over time
- Don't overuse the same attention-grabbing technique
- Novelty wears off—substance must follow

---

## What Motivates People

### The Goal Gradient Effect

**Motivation increases as people approach a goal.** The closer the finish line, the harder people work.

**Design implications:**
- Show progress clearly (progress bars)
- Make users feel closer to completion than they are
- Artificial advancement works (2/12 stamps given free feels better than 0/10)

---

### Variable Rewards

**Unpredictable rewards are more compelling than predictable ones.** This is why slot machines and social media are addictive.

**Design implications:**
- Variable rewards drive engagement (new content, likes)
- Use ethically—this can be manipulative
- Predictable rewards are better for sustainable motivation

---

### Dopamine Drives Seeking

**Dopamine is about wanting, not having.** It motivates the search, not the satisfaction. The anticipation is more exciting than the reward.

**Design implications:**
- Tease what's coming to build anticipation
- The reveal should match or exceed expectations
- Endless scrolling exploits dopamine loops

---

### Intrinsic > Extrinsic Motivation

**Internal motivation beats external rewards.** Autonomy, mastery, and purpose are more sustainable than money or prizes.

**Design implications:**
- Help users feel competent and in control
- Provide meaningful choices
- External rewards can actually decrease intrinsic motivation

---

### Progress, Mastery, Control

**People are motivated by:**
1. **Progress** - Sense of advancement
2. **Mastery** - Becoming better at something
3. **Control** - Autonomy over their experience

**Design implications:**
- Show users they're improving
- Celebrate milestones
- Give users meaningful choices (even if outcomes are similar)

---

## How People Are Social

### Social Validation

**People look to others for guidance.** Especially when uncertain. This is why testimonials, reviews, and "X people bought this" work.

**Design implications:**
- Show what others have done/chosen
- Display social proof (reviews, ratings, user counts)
- Highlight popular options

---

### The Need to Belong

**Belonging is a fundamental human need.** We're wired to fit in with groups.

**Design implications:**
- Create community features
- Show users they're part of a group
- Use inclusive language ("Join 10,000 members")

---

### Dunbar's Number: ~150

**People maintain about 150 stable relationships.** Beyond that, social connections become weaker.

**Design implications:**
- Design for manageable social networks
- Quality > quantity for meaningful connections
- Large follower counts don't equal real relationships

---

### Real vs. Fake Smiles

**People can tell genuine from fake smiles.** Real smiles (Duchenne smiles) involve the eyes; fake smiles only move the mouth.

**Design implications:**
- Use authentic imagery with real expressions
- Avoid obvious stock photos with fake smiles
- Authenticity builds trust

---

## How People Decide

### Too Many Choices Paralyze

**Choice overload leads to decision paralysis.** The famous jam study: 24 options = 3% purchase rate; 6 options = 30% purchase rate.

**Design implications:**
- Limit choices to ~4 primary options
- Provide recommendations/defaults
- Use progressive disclosure for advanced options

---

### Illusion of Choice

**People want to feel in control even with limited options.** Offering variations (colors, sizes) satisfies this without overwhelming.

**Design implications:**
- Offer variations within a category vs. many different categories
- "Choose your color" feels empowering
- Constrained choices can feel like freedom

---

### Emotion Drives Decisions

**Without emotion, people can't decide.** Patients with damaged emotional centers have intact logic but can't make simple choices.

**Design implications:**
- Don't rely purely on rational arguments
- Evoke appropriate emotions
- Emotional design isn't manipulative—it's necessary

---

### Look and Feel = Trust

**Visual design is the first indicator of trust.** People judge credibility in 50 milliseconds based on appearance.

**Design implications:**
- Professional appearance = trustworthy
- Design quality signals product quality
- First impressions are hard to overcome

---

## How People Make Errors

### Swiss Cheese Model

**Errors happen when multiple small failures align.** Like holes in Swiss cheese layers lining up.

**Design implications:**
- Build multiple safeguards
- Don't rely on one error prevention method
- Assume some errors will slip through each layer

---

### Expect Errors, Design for Recovery

**People will make mistakes.** The question is: how easy is recovery?

**Design implications:**
- Make undo available
- Provide clear error messages with solutions
- Never permanently destroy data without confirmation

---

### Stress Increases Errors

**Under stress, people make more mistakes.** High-stakes situations = more cognitive load = more errors.

**Design implications:**
- Simplify high-stress interactions (checkout, forms)
- Reduce cognitive load in critical paths
- Provide reassurance and clarity

## Audit Checklist: Psychology Principles

### Vision & Attention
- [ ] Is critical info in the central focus area?
- [ ] Is movement/animation intentional and minimal?
- [ ] Do faces look toward key content?
- [ ] Are related items visually grouped?
- [ ] Is red/blue combination avoided?

### Reading & Memory
- [ ] Is body text ≥16px?
- [ ] Is line length 45-75 characters?
- [ ] Are there ~4 or fewer navigation items?
- [ ] Is recognition used over recall?
- [ ] Is content chunked into digestible pieces?

### Attention & Motivation
- [ ] Is there one clear primary action per view?
- [ ] Is progress shown clearly?
- [ ] Are users given meaningful choices?
- [ ] Is social proof present?
- [ ] Are intrinsic motivators leveraged?

### Decisions & Errors
- [ ] Are choices limited to prevent paralysis?
- [ ] Is there a clear default/recommendation?
- [ ] Does visual design build trust?
- [ ] Are errors easy to recover from?
- [ ] Are high-stress flows simplified?

### Social & Emotional
- [ ] Is authentic imagery used?
- [ ] Is there social validation/proof?
- [ ] Does the design evoke appropriate emotion?
- [ ] Is the experience inclusive?
